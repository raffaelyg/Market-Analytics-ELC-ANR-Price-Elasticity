## The Challenge

A globally iconic prestige serum — one of the category's most recognised products — was experiencing growth stagnation in a major APAC market. On paper, the buyer base was still expanding. But value growth had flatlined. The client's executive team needed to understand why more buyers weren't translating into more revenue, and what to do about it.

The conventional explanation was competitive pressure. I suspected the answer was behavioural — something in how consumers were buying, not just what they were buying.

---

## The "One Question"

> What specific behavioural friction is preventing buyer base growth from converting into value growth — and is our own promotional strategy contributing to the problem?

---

## My Role

This was a 45-day consulting sprint. I was the lead analyst, responsible for:
- Reframing the client's "why aren't we growing" question into a testable behavioural hypothesis
- Designing the analytical framework combining price elasticity modelling with buyer outflow and promotion habit analysis
- Integrating continuous purchase panel data with competitive switching data at the individual buyer level
- Delivering the strategic diagnosis and recovery roadmap to the brand's senior leadership

---

## Analytical Approach

### The "Say-Do" Gap in Pricing Sensitivity

Using Kantar Worldpanel single-source data, I modelled the gap between stated price sensitivity and actual purchasing behaviour. Consumers who claimed to be loyal and price-insensitive were, in practice, exhibiting a very different pattern — one shaped by years of promotional conditioning.

### Three Invisible Barriers

Through the analysis, I identified three interconnected barriers that traditional brand health metrics had missed:

**1. Traction Centre Shift.** The product category that had historically recruited new prestige buyers was losing momentum to an adjacent category. The "entry point" into prestige was migrating, and the brand's portfolio was still anchored to the old one.

**2. Promotion Habit Loop.** Years of aggressive promotional activity had conditioned consumers to purchase only during discount windows. This extended the time between purchases, depressing value growth even as the buyer base expanded. The brand's own promotional strategy was training buyers to wait.

**3. Competitive "Job-to-be-Done" Reframing.** Emerging domestic competitors weren't just cheaper — they had reframed the functional promise from general maintenance to specific, results-oriented efficacy. They were being "hired" for a different job, one the prestige brand wasn't competing on.

### Price Elasticity & SKU Optimisation

The Data Science Team and I collaboratively built a price sensitivity model using purchase panel data to identify the optimal price threshold — the point that maximises revenue per buyer without triggering catastrophic volume loss. I also analysed SKU-level purchase behaviour to determine which pack size drove the strongest long-term loyalty (repeat purchase frequency and retention), versus which pack sizes encouraged stockpiling behaviour that depressed repurchase rates.

## What Made This Project Effective

1. **Behavioural framing over brand health metrics.** The client had plenty of tracking data showing "brand health is fine." The value I added was asking a different question: what are consumers *doing* that contradicts what they're *saying*? That reframe uncovered the promotion habit loop — the most actionable finding in the entire engagement.

2. **Connecting promotion strategy to growth stagnation.** This was a politically sensitive finding. Telling a brand team that their own promotions are part of the problem requires the data to be airtight. I built the evidence base at the individual buyer level — showing the time-between-purchase elongation correlated with promotional intensity — so the recommendation was undeniable.

3. **31 days, complete narrative.** This was a sprint engagement with a tight turnaround. The deliverable was not just data — it was a complete strategic narrative: diagnosis, evidence, and a recovery roadmap with specific pricing, SKU, and positioning actions. My principle: an insight that cannot be acted on within the next planning cycle is not useful.
